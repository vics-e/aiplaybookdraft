The AI Playbook for Accountants & Bookkeepers
From assistants to agents: building the AI-ready firm
Artificial intelligence is now part of everyday accountancy.
This playbook is designed to help firms move from experimentation to structured, confident adoption.

Page 2 - Introduction: The Shift to AI-Assisted Firms
Over the past two decades, the accounting profession has experienced several major technology shifts.
First came desktop software.
Then cloud accounting transformed collaboration.
Automation reduced manual data entry and repetitive processing.
Now, we are entering the next phase: AI-assisted firms.
Artificial intelligence is not about replacing accountants.
It is about removing repetitive effort, improving consistency, and giving professionals more time to focus on judgement, advice, and client relationships.
Many firms have already experimented with AI:
•	drafting emails,
•	summarising notes,
•	producing explanations,
•	or generating checklists.
But experimentation alone does not create real change.
What matters is structured adoption, built around:
•	workflows,
•	safeguards,
•	and measurable outcomes.

Even small efficiency improvements compound quickly.
If routine tasks take 30–50% less time, that can translate into hours back each month across a team, reducing pressure during peak periods and creating space for higher-value work.
This playbook will help you:
•	adopt AI safely,
•	apply it to real workflows,
•	protect professional judgement,
•	and create sustainable capacity inside your firm.
The firms that succeed with AI will not be the ones who experiment the most.
They will be the ones who turn time saved into better outcomes - for clients and for their teams.

Page 3 - The Three Stages of AI in Accountancy
AI adoption in accounting firms is moving through three clear stages.
Stage 1 - Automation
Rules-based systems perform repetitive tasks:
•	bank feeds,
•	automated postings,
•	OCR and data capture,
•	scheduled processes.
Automation follows fixed rules.
It is predictable, but limited.

Stage 2 - Assistants
Generative AI tools help with individual tasks:
•	drafting emails,
•	summarising information,
•	explaining figures,
•	preparing checklists.
Assistants are powerful, but they rely on:
•	prompts,
•	context,
•	and human direction.
They help with tasks, not full processes.

Stage 3 - Agents
AI agents operate with a defined goal and set of constraints. They can:
•	performing multiple steps,
•	operating inside accounting systems, 
•	make decisions with defined policies,
•	and presenting results for approval. 
Agents are not just step-driven like traditional automation.
They are goal-driven, working within guardrails to reach an outcome.
All agent activity should include a human-in-the-loop review point before final outputs are used or submitted.
Instead of only helping with one task, agents can take responsibility for entire processes or defined outcomes.

Core principle
Assistants respond.
Agents act.

Page 4 - The Sage AI Practice Framework
Based on professional guidance, real firm experiences, and emerging best practice, Sage has developed a simple framework for the AI-ready firm.
The Five Principles of the AI-Ready Firm
1) Start with the workflow
AI works best when:
•	processes are defined,
•	steps are repeatable,
•	outcomes are predictable.
Automation executes predefined steps.
Assistants respond to prompts.
Agents pursue outcomes by determining the steps required within defined guardrails. 

2) Keep the accountant in control
AI may:
•	draft,
•	check,
•	summarise,
•	or prepare.
But:
•	the accountant reviews,
•	applies judgement,
•	signs off and presses send – the one in control. 
All AI-assisted work should follow a human-in-the-loop model, where the professional remains accountable for the result.
Responsibility always remains with the professional. 

3) Trust structured data over clever prompts
AI output quality depends on: 
•	the data,
•	the context,
•	the process behind it,
•	and the quality of the instructions provided
Clear prompts improve results, but clean, structured records and defined workflows creates error free, reliable AI outcomes. 

4) Turn capacity into value
AI creates time.  
Firms must decide how to use it.
The capacity can be used to:
•	improve margins,
•	deliver a better client experience,
•	deepen client relationships,
•	take on new clients,
•	or introduce new advisory services.
The most successful firms reinvest the AI dividend into client value and long term growth.

5) Build for agents, not just assistants
Today:
•	AI helps with individual tasks.
Tomorrow:
•	AI systems will take responsibility for defined outcomes across workflows.
Firms should: 
•	design clear processes,
•	define outcomes and review points,
•	and stay open to new ways of working.
Do not just experiment with prompts.
Build structured, agent-ready processes

Page 7 - Assistants vs Agents: The New Way Work Gets Done
Understanding the difference between assistants and agents is critical.

Assistant-led work
The accountant:
•	performs the process,
•	uses AI to help with tasks,
•	and remains in control throughout.
Examples:
•	drafting emails,
•	summarising notes,
•	preparing explanations.
In this model, the assistant responds to instructions, and the human performs the overall process.

Agent-led work
The agent:
•	works toward a defined goal or outcome,
•	determines the steps required within set guardrails,
•	operates across multiple actions,
•	and prepares the result for review.
The accountant:
•	reviews the outcome,
•	applies judgement,
•	approves, 
•	and sends or finalises the result.
All agent-led work should follow a human-in-the-loop model.

The key shift
Old model:
Human performs tasks → time spent.
Assistant model:
Human performs the process → AI helps with tasks
Agent model:
Agent works toward the outcome → human reviews and approves

Page 9 - Professional Ethics and Responsibility in an AI World
Using AI does not change the ethical responsibilities of accountants.
Professional codes of ethics and tax conduct guidance, such as those within the Professional Conduct in Relation to Taxation (PCRT) framework, make it clear that responsibility for work remains with the accountant, regardless of the technology used.
The fundamental principles still apply:
•	integrity,
•	objectivity,
•	professional competence and due care,
•	confidentiality,
•	professional behaviour.
AI may assist the process, but:
•	the professional signs off the work,
•	the professional has the final say and pushes to submit, 
•	the firm remains accountable, 
•	and judgement cannot be delegated to technology.

The practical rule
If you would not sign it without checking it,
you should not send it.

Page 10 - The Human-in-the-Loop Model
AI works best as part of a controlled, supervised process.
This is known as the human-in-the-loop model.

The workflow
Step 1 - Assistant or agent
AI:
•	collects information,
•	performs checks,
•	drafts outputs,
•	prepares summaries.

Step 2 - Accountant review
The accountant:
•	reviews the results,
•	applies judgement,
•	makes adjustments.

Step 3 - Accountant approval
The accountant:
•	signs off the final output,
•	sends it to the client or authority,
•	and takes responsibility.

Why this matters
This model:
•	protects quality,
•	preserves accountability,
•	aligns with professional standards,
•	and builds confidence in AI use.
AI performs the steps.
The accountant remains accountable.

Page 11 - Professional Principles for AI in Accountancy
Artificial intelligence does not change the professional responsibilities of accountants.
It simply changes how work is produced.
Professional codes of ethics and tax conduct guidance, including frameworks such as the Professional Conduct in Relation to Taxation (PCRT), make it clear that responsibility always remains with the accountant and the firm.
Technology may assist the work.
It does not sign it off.

The professional foundations of AI use
Any use of AI in an accounting firm must respect the core ethical principles:
•	Integrity
Be honest and straightforward in all professional work.
•	Objectivity
Do not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence.
•	Professional competence and due care
 Maintain knowledge and skill, and review work properly.
•	Confidentiality
Protect client and firm information at all times.
•	Professional behaviour
Comply with laws, regulations, and professional standards.

Core message
AI can assist the process.
The professional remains responsible for the outcome.

2.	Can I justify the output?

This happens when:
•	staff use unapproved tools,
•	client data is pasted into personal accounts,

•	no audit trail is kept,
•	different people use AI in different ways.

What this leads to
Unstructured AI use creates:
•	inconsistent work,
•	data risks,

•	and loss of professional confidence.

Why structured workflows matter
Accountants trust:
•	checklists,
•	defined processes,
•	and repeatable steps.
AI works best when:
•	it follows those same structures,
•	inside defined workflows,
•	with clear, human review points.

Page 14 - Data and Confidentiality in an AI World
Accountants handle sensitive information every day.
AI does not change that responsibility.

The three-level data model
Level 1 - Public data
•	General questions
•	Public tax rates
•	Industry insights
Safe for most AI tools.

Level 2 - Internal firm data
•	Templates
•	Procedures
•	Internal notes
Use only with approved tools.

Level 3 - Client confidential data
•	Financial records
•	Personal information
•	Tax data
Only use:
•	inside approved systems,
•	or AI tools embedded within accounting platforms.

The three-question test
Before using AI, ask:
1.	Would I email this data to a stranger?
2.	Would I store this in a public document?
3.	Is this the minimum information needed?
If any answer is no, do not use it.

If AI produces:

•	misleading advice,

The fact that AI was used:

•	and client expectations.
Some clients may:

Page 16 - Common AI Failure Modes
AI systems can produce convincing outputs that are still incorrect.
Understanding common failure modes helps firms manage risk.

The most common AI issues
1) Confident errors
AI can produce  incorrect answers that sound authoritative.

2) Fabricated information
AI may:
•	invent facts,
•	create false citations,
•	or reference non-existent sources.

3) Missing context
AI may:
•	give generic advice,
•	ignore important details,
•	or apply the wrong assumptions.

4) Bias or skewed results
Outputs may reflect:
•	incomplete data,
•	outdated assumptions,
•	or unbalanced perspectives.

Core message
AI outputs must always be reviewed with professional scepticism.

Page 17 - Avoiding Over-Reliance on AI
One of the biggest risks is not technical.
It is behavioural.
When AI produces confident outputs, people may:
•	trust it too quickly,
•	skip review steps,
•	or stop questioning results.

Signs of over-reliance
Watch for:
•	staff copying outputs without checking,
•	reduced critical thinking,
•	fewer questions being asked,
•	increased confidence with no evidence.

The professional mindset
AI should be treated as:
•	a junior assistant,
•	or a draft-producing tool.
Not:
•	a final decision-maker,
•	or an authority.

Practical rule
If you cannot explain the output,
you should not rely on it.

Activity: Scepticism habit

“What would we check before trusting this output?”
Make that the standard review step.

Page 20 - The Red-Team Exercise
One of the best ways to build confidence in AI is to test its weaknesses.
This is known as a red-team exercise.
Instead of asking:
“Where does AI help us?”
Ask:
“Where could this go wrong?”

How to run a red-team exercise
1.	Choose a simple, real task.
2.	Give AI limited or unclear information.
3.	Observe the output.
4.	Identify:
o	errors,
o	assumptions,
o	or risks.
5.	Write down what controls would prevent this.

Why this matters
This exercise:
•	builds scepticism,
•	improves policies,
•	and strengthens workflows.
It turns AI risk into practical learning.

Action: Run your first red-team test
Choose one task:
•	email drafting,
•	tax explanation,
•	or client summary.
Test AI with:
•	incomplete information,
•	or a vague prompt.
Then write:
•	two risks,
•	and two controls to prevent them.

Page 24 - The Agent Maturity Ladder
AI adoption usually follows a clear progression.

The five stages of AI maturity
Stage 1 - Prompts
Staff use AI to answer questions or draft text.
Work is still entirely human-led.

Stage 2 - Assistants
AI is used regularly for defined tasks.
It helps with drafting, checking, and summarising, while the accountant remains in control.

Stage 3 - Workflow packs
Prompts, checklists, and processes are standardised across the firm.
Work becomes more consistent and repeatable.

Stage 4 - Agents
AI operates inside systems to achieve defined goals or outputs.

Page 25 - The Workflow Map: Where Agents Work Best
Not every process should be automated.
Agents work best where:

•	the structure of the process is high,
•	the steps are repeatable,
•	and the required judgement is limited or well-bounded.
In these situations, an agent can decide how to reach the outcome by following the available data, rules, and system constraints.

Agents require greater oversight where:

•	the work is highly judgement-driven,
•	the data is incomplete or unstructured,

Page 36 - The Accountant’s Prompt Framework
AI assistants work best when instructions are:
•	clear,
•	structured,
•	and purposeful.
A vague question produces a vague answer.
A structured prompt produces a useful result.

The Sage Prompt Framework
Every effective prompt has four parts:
1) Role
Who the AI should act as.
Example:
•	“You are an accountant…”
•	“You are a payroll specialist…”
•	“You are a client relationship manager…”

2) Task
What you want it to do.
Example:
•	Draft an email
•	Summarise a report
•	Explain a variance
•	Prepare a checklist

3) Context
The situation, background, or data.
Example:
•	Type of client
•	Industry
•	Financial figures
•	Previous discussion points

4) Constraints
Any rules the output must follow.
Example:
•	Word limit
•	Tone
•	Structure
•	Compliance considerations

Simple prompt structure
Role + Task + Context + Constraints

Example prompt
“You are an accountant.
Draft a short, professional email to a retail client explaining that their quarterly profit has decreased due to higher supplier costs.
Keep it under 120 words and use a supportive tone.”

Page 65 - AI Glossary
A quick reference to common AI terms.

AI (Artificial Intelligence)
Technology that performs tasks normally requiring human intelligence, such as analysing data or generating text.

AI Agent
An AI system that:
•	works towards a defined goal or outcome,
•	can perform multiple steps across systems or data sources,
•	makes decisions within set rules or constraints,
•	and prepares results for human review and approval.
Agents focus on achieving outcomes, not just completing individual tasks

AI Audit Trail 
A record showing:
•	how AI was used,
•	what outputs were generated,
•	and who approved the final result.

AI Assistant
An AI tool that helps with individual tasks, such as:
•	drafting emails,
•	summarising notes,
•	or explaining figures.
Assistants respond to prompts and require human direction.

AI dividend
The time and efficiency gained from using AI and agents.

AI Policy
A firm’s internal rules governing:
•	how AI can be used,
•	what data is allowed,
•	and what review is required.

Generative AI 
AI that creates new content, such as:
•	text,
•	summaries,
•	explanations,
•	code,
•	images,
•	audio,
•	or video.
Generative AI produces original outputs based on patterns learned from large sets of training data

Audit trail
A record showing:
•	what was done,
•	how it was done,
•	and who approved it.

Automation
Technology that performs tasks using fixed rules and processes, without human intervention.
Examples:
•	bank feeds,
•	recurring journals,
•	rules-based expense coding.
Automation is different from generative AI, which creates new content.

Bias (in AI)
When an AI system produces skewed or unfair results because of:
•	incomplete data,
•	unbalanced training,
•	or incorrect assumptions.

Context
The background information provided to an AI system.
Better context usually leads to:
•	more accurate,
•	more relevant,
•	and more useful outputs.

Context Window
The amount of information an AI system can consider at one time when producing a response.
Larger context windows allow AI to:
•	analyse longer documents,
•	or consider more data at once.

Data Minimisation
The principle of only sharing the minimum amount of data needed to complete a task.

Fine-Tuning
The process of adjusting an AI model using specific data to improve performance for a particular task or industry.

Hallucination (AI)
When an AI system produces:
•	incorrect,
•	fabricated,
•	or misleading information
that appears confident and believable.
This is why all AI outputs must be reviewed.

Human-in-the-loop
A process where:
•	AI performs tasks,
•	and a human reviews and approves the outcome.

LLM (Large Language Model)
A type of AI trained on large amounts of text data.
It can understand language, generate responses, summarise information, and assist with writing or analysis.
Most modern AI assistants are powered by LLMs.

Machine Learning (ML)
A type of AI where systems learn patterns from data and improve over time without being explicitly programmed for every rule.
Used in:
•	fraud detection,
•	expense categorisation,
•	and forecasting tools.

Model
The AI system itself, trained on data to perform tasks such as:
•	prediction,
•	classification,
•	or content generation.

Natural Language Processing (NLP)
Technology that allows computers to:
•	understand,
•	interpret,
•	and generate human language.
NLP enables AI tools to:
•	read emails,
•	interpret text,
•	and answer questions.

Prompt
The instruction given to an AI system.

Prompt Engineering
The practice of writing clear, structured prompts to improve AI results.
This includes:
•	defining the role,
•	setting the task,
•	providing context,
•	and adding constraints.

Structured data
Organised, consistent information that AI systems can process reliably.

Training Data
The information used to teach an AI Model  how to:
•	recognise patterns,
•	generate responses,
•	and perform tasks.
The quality of the training data affects the quality of the results.

Workflow
A defined sequence of steps used to complete a task or service.
